About

Kate Francke

Technologist and Translator

Hi, I'm Kate Francke, a recent dual degree graduate from Columbia University and Sciences Po, where I studied Computer Science and Economics. But unlike most of my classmates, I wasn't drawn to coding because of an innate love of logic. I came to it hesitantly: I chose these fields because they increasingly dictate everything else. Attention economies shape what we believe. Automated systems decide who gets housing, healthcare, bail. Consumerism is now an algorithm.

This intersection of technology with our human lives cannot be overlooked, but we shouldn't all need to understand language models, linked lists, and binary code to use these tools safely or know when they're being misused. I studied computer science to act as a translator for the people affected by these systems but not consulted in building them.

The questions I'm most interested in right now:

  • How do we build technology that works for public good, and who gets to decide what that means?
  • How do we bring these tools to the communities that need them most, without the tools themselves becoming another source of harm? For instance, how can we avoid reinforcing datasets built on skewed histories, embedding knowledge systems that mistake Western defaults for universal truth, and enabling attention economies that profit from isolation and outrage?
  • When a handful of private companies control the infrastructure of public life, how do we keep them accountable?

I'm a technologist in training, but when I'm not typing away at my computer, I'm playing rugby, practicing my paradiddles on the drums, or dreaming about my future surf career (I can't surf yet but I'm fully bought into the lifestyle).

Research

Research Assistant, CAIAC Model Diffing Group

Columbia University  ·  January – May 2025

What actually changes inside a neural network when you train it differently? I worked on analyzing attention mechanisms in transformer models before and after GRPO fine-tuning, using PyTorch to visualize how information flows through layers, track structural shifts, and examine induction heads. Unfortunately, we didn't publish any work, as the project turned out to be more ambitious than the timeline allowed. Still, I came away with a working understanding of transformers and attention mechanisms, a clearer sense of how research actually gets structured and delegated, and a firsthand experience of the technical barriers to understanding AI. The layers of detail are genuinely overwhelming, and a semester barely scratches the surface. But it was enough to understand why translation matters, and to develop a real respect for how much technical depth this work requires.

Columbia AI Alignment Fellow

Columbia University  ·  Spring 2025

A structured reading group working through a curated AI safety curriculum covering catastrophic risks, interpretability techniques, and governance frameworks. We met weekly to read and discuss material. We covered many topics, but one paper that stuck with me was Sastry et al.'s Computing Power and the Governance of Artificial Intelligence, which discussed how compute might be one of the few places where regulation is actually feasible. Unlike data or algorithms, compute runs on chips in data centers, which gives it a physical footprint that can, in theory, be monitored and controlled. But the paper also highlights why this might be harder than at first glance. When a small number of companies control compute, they also control who gets to build AI systems, what it gets used for, and who can audit it. Meaningful oversight would require access to their data centers, their training logs, their internal processes, and the legal frameworks we have were not built to compel that kind of technical transparency from companies operating at this scale, across borders, and faster than most legislation can move.

Technical Projects

OceanTemp PCA Analysis

Python  ·  Jupyter

800,000 years of climate history, stored in Antarctic ice cores as trace concentrations of CO₂, methane, dust, and isotopes. This was a group project for our linear algebra class, using Principal Component Analysis to reconstruct global mean ocean temperatures from those proxy measurements.

Beautiful Venmo

Python  ·  FastAPI

An decentralized payment ledger for tracking debts and payments. It establishes a peer to peer etwork to record and secure transactions on a blockchain. Users can submit payments and requests on the application which will then be securely stored on the blockchain using cryptographic signatures. Team project.

Reliable Transport Protocol

Python

This project rebuilds the internet's delivery guarantees (that my file will arive complete and in order) from scratch over UDP: a three-way handshake to open connections, a sliding window to pace data, checksums to catch corruption, and automatic retransmission when packets go missing. In short, a ground-up implementation of how TCP works.

Distance Vector Routing

Python

Shows how data find its way across the internet when no single node can see the whole map. This project implements the Bellman-Ford routing algorithm: each node knows only its immediate neighbors, but by continuously sharing and comparing routing tables, the whole network converges on the cheapest path between any two points.

Adaptive Bitrate Streaming

Python

Video streaming that adjusts quality based on connection speed. Basically demonstrates why YouTube drops to 480p when Wi-Fi gets spotty. This client uses an exponentially weighted moving average to estimate available bandwidth in real time, then selects the highest-quality chunk that fits.
